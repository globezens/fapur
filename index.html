Purple is closely associated with violet. In common usage, both refer to colors that are between red and blue in hue, but depending on the cultural and geographical background of authors and speakers the purples may or may not be placed closer to red and violets closer to blue. Similarly, in the traditional painters' color wheel, purple and violet are both placed between red and blue, with purple closer or not closer to red. In the United Kingdom, many native speakers of English refer to the blue-dominated spectral color beyond blue as violet, but this color is called purple by many speakers in the United States. In some texts the term violet refers to any color between red and blue. However, there are also authoritative texts from the United Kingdom in which this same range of colors is referred to by the term purple. When including languages other than English, and epochs other than the modern period, the uncertainty about the meanings of the color terms violet and purple is even larger. This CIE chromaticity diagram highlights the line of purples at its base, running from the violet corner near the left to the red corner at the right. In textbooks on optics, violet is often considered to be a spectral color: It may then refer to the color of any different single wavelength of light on the short wavelength end of the visible spectrum, between approximately 380 and 450 nanometers. To some native speakers of English, purple is the color of various combinations of red, blue, and violet light; to other native speakers of English, the same range of colors is referred to as violet .
